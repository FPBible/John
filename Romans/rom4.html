Romans 4

Abraham Fulfilled, Promise Realised

Original text: Romans 4 — WEB

Immediate Context

Romans 4 explains how righteousness apart from the Law was always God’s intention. Paul does not introduce a new method of salvation. He shows that the covenant shift now revealed in Christ was anticipated in Abraham himself.

This chapter is not about abstract faith. It is about covenant inheritance reaching its appointed fulfillment.

Fulfilled Paraphrase

Abraham Counted Righteous Before the Law (4:1–8)

What then shall we say about Abraham, the forefather of Israel according to the flesh?

If Abraham had been justified by works, he would have had something to boast about — but not before God.

For what did the Scripture say? “Abraham believed God, and it was counted to him as righteousness.”

Now wages are not credited as a gift, but as something owed.

But to the one who does not work yet trusts the God who justifies the ungodly, faith was counted as righteousness.

David spoke of this same blessing, describing the person whose lawless deeds were forgiven and whose sins were covered.

Fulfilled clarification: Righteousness is credited apart from Law, and this principle was already active before Sinai existed.

Before Circumcision, Before Boundary (4:9–12)

Was this blessing only for the circumcised? Or also for the uncircumcised?

Faith was counted to Abraham as righteousness before he was circumcised.

Circumcision was later given as a sign and seal of the righteousness he already possessed by faith.

This made Abraham the father of all who believe — both the uncircumcised who believe and the circumcised who do not rely on the Law.

Fulfilled clarification: Circumcision never created covenant righteousness. It marked a role now fulfilled and surpassed in Christ.

The Promise Fulfilled Apart from the Law (4:13–17)

The promise to Abraham that he would inherit the world did not come through the Law, but through the righteousness of faith.

If inheritance depended on the Law, faith would be emptied and the promise nullified.

The Law produced transgression, because where the Law existed, violation followed.

For this reason, the promise rested on grace, so that it would be guaranteed to all Abraham’s offspring — not only those under the Law, but those who shared Abraham’s faith.

As it is written: “I have made you a father of many nations.”

Fulfilled clarification: “Inherit the world” refers to covenant authority and inclusion, now realised through Christ, not a postponed global possession.

Life Out of Death — Then and Now (4:18–22)

Against human expectation, Abraham trusted in God and became the father of many nations, just as it had been spoken.

He did not deny the reality of his own lifeless body or Sarah’s barrenness,

yet he did not waver in unbelief, but was strengthened in faith, giving glory to God.

He was fully convinced that God was able to do what He had promised.

That is why his faith was counted as righteousness.

Fulfilled clarification: Abraham’s story foreshadows covenant resurrection — life emerging where the Law could not produce it.

The Promise Completed in Christ (4:23–25)

These words — “it was counted to him” — were not written for Abraham alone,

but for us as well, to whom righteousness was credited,

because we trusted the God who raised Jesus our Lord from the dead.

He was handed over because of transgressions and raised to establish our justification.

Fulfilled clarification: Justification is not pending. The resurrection publicly confirmed the righteous status now shared by those in Christ.

Fulfillment Commentary

1) Abraham proves righteousness predates the Law

Paul uses Abraham to dismantle the idea that covenant standing was ever earned by obedience.

2) “Inheriting the world” is covenantal

This promise speaks of scope, not geography — the expansion of God’s family beyond Israel.

3) Resurrection language is corporate

Life from death describes the emergence of a new covenant people, not a delayed biological event.

4) Christ completes the Abrahamic promise

The resurrection marks the public moment when righteousness was confirmed apart from the Law for all who believe.

New Creation Reality

Faith is not waiting. It is participation.

Abraham’s promise is no longer anticipated. It is realised in a people formed by the Spirit, not by Law or lineage.

Believers live from a completed promise, not toward an uncertain future.

Reflection

Where might I still treat God’s promise as something to earn or wait for, instead of something already given in Christ?
