Preface

How to Read Ephesians

Ephesians is a letter of identity and formation. Paul is not trying to scare believers into compliance. He is showing them what is already true in Christ — and then teaching them how to live from it together.

This letter reads like a declaration: you are in Christ, you are reconciled, you are part of one new humanity, and you are seated in a new realm of authority and access.

What Ephesians Is Doing

  • Identity: blessing, adoption, inheritance, sealing, and access in Christ
  • Unity: Jew and Gentile formed into one body without covenant walls
  • Formation: how the new humanity speaks, forgives, works, and loves
  • Vocation: the church as God’s public display of wisdom in the world
  • Stability: standing firm without fear-based spirituality

How This Fits the Fulfilled / New Creation Lens

Ephesians assumes the decisive reconciliation has been completed in Christ. It speaks from the reality of a new covenant people being formed as the old boundary system loses its authority.

You will see covenant transition themes throughout the letter:

  • language of “inheritance” and “sealed” identity
  • the breaking down of the dividing wall
  • new creation ethics replacing rule-based spirituality
  • the collapse of fear-driven “powers” thinking

Ephesians does not invite believers to wait for a future status. It calls them to live from a present status: you are already brought near, already reconciled, already included.

Key Phrases to Watch

  • “In Christ” — union as the basis of everything
  • “Heavenly places” — realm of authority and access, not geography
  • “One new humanity” — a new people formed beyond covenant boundaries
  • “Walk” — lived identity (speech, relationships, work, love)
  • “Powers and authorities” — intimidation structures under Christ’s rule

How to Use the Commentary

The paraphrase sections aim to stay close to the flow of the text in clear modern language. The commentary sections then make the implications explicit: what Paul meant then, what had changed in Christ, and what New Creation life looks like now.

If you are new to this framework: read the chapter first without the commentary. Then return to the commentary and cross-references.

An Invitation

Ephesians is not written to produce spiritual striving. It is written to produce grounded confidence.

Read slowly. Let Paul’s repeated emphasis land: in Christ, you are already blessed, already brought near, already included.

Then let the “walk” sections do their work — not as burdens, but as the natural shape of the new humanity.
