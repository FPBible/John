Matthew 1

The Genesis of the New Covenant

Immediate Context

Matthew 1 functions as a legal document. It establishes Jesus not just as a biological descendant, but as the terminal point of Israel’s history.

The silence of the intertestamental period is broken. The countdown to the end of the Old Age has begun.

Scripture (LSV)

1 A roll of the birth of Jesus Christ, son of David, son of Abraham.

2 Abraham begat Isaac, and Isaac begat Jacob, and Jacob begat Judah and his brethren, 3 and Judah begat Pharez and Zarah of Tamar, and Pharez begat Hezron, and Hezron begat Ram, 4 and Ram begat Amminadab, and Amminadab begat Nahshon, and Nahshon begat Salmon, 5 and Salmon begat Boaz of Rahab, and Boaz begat Obed of Ruth, and Obed begat Jesse, 6 and Jesse begat David the king. And David the king begat Solomon, of her [who had been] Uriah’s,

7 and Solomon begat Rehoboam, and Rehoboam begat Abijah, and Abijah begat Asa, 8 and Asa begat Jehoshaphat, and Jehoshaphat begat Joram, and Joram begat Uzziah, 9 and Uzziah begat Jotham, and Jotham begat Ahaz, and Ahaz begat Hezekiah, 10 and Hezekiah begat Manasseh, and Manasseh begat Amon, and Amon begat Josiah, 11 and Josiah begat Jeconiah and his brethren, at the Babylonian removal.

12 And after the Babylonian removal, Jeconiah begat Shealtiel, and Shealtiel begat Zerubbabel, 13 and Zerubbabel begat Abiud, and Abiud begat Eliakim, and Eliakim begat Azor, 14 and Azor begat Sadoc, and Sadoc begat Achim, and Achim begat Eliud, 15 and Eliud begat Eleazar, and Eleazar begat Matthan, and Matthan begat Jacob, 16 and Jacob begat Joseph, the husband of Mary, of whom was begotten Jesus, who is named Christ.

17 All the generations, therefore, from Abraham to David [are] fourteen generations, and from David to the Babylonian removal fourteen generations, and from the Babylonian removal to the Christ fourteen generations.

18 And of Jesus Christ, the birth was thus: For his mother Mary having been betrothed to Joseph, before their coming together she was found having [conception] in the womb from the Holy Spirit, 19 and Joseph her husband being righteous, and not willing to make her an example, did wish privately to send her away.

20 And on his thinking of these things, lo, a messenger of the LORD in a dream appeared to him, saying, “Joseph, son of David, thou mayest not fear to receive Mary thy wife, for that which in her was begotten [is] of the Holy Spirit, 21 and she shall bring forth a son, and thou shalt call his name Jesus, for he shall save his people from their sins.”

22 And all this hath come to pass, that it might be fulfilled that was spoken by the Lord through the prophet, saying, 23 “Lo, the virgin shall conceive, and she shall bring forth a son, and they shall call his name Emmanuel,” which is, being interpreted, “With us [is] God.”

24 And Joseph, having risen from the sleep, did as the messenger of the LORD directed him, and received his wife, 25 and did not know her till she brought forth her son—the first-born—and he called his name Jesus.

Annotations

1:17 — Babylonian removal: Literally "deportation" or "exile." Marks the covenantal timeline of Israel’s judgment.
1:21 — His people: Contextually restricted to Israel (the covenant people).
1:21 — Their sins: Refers to the covenant-breaking trespasses that caused the Exile, rather than generic moral failure.
1:23 — With us [is] God: Signifies the restoration of God’s presence returning to His people, replacing the need for a physical Temple.

Fulfillment Commentary

1) A legal claim secured before the fire

Matthew opens his Gospel not with a biography, but with a legal brief. To a first-century Jewish audience, a King could not simply appear; he had to be proven. This genealogy establishes Jesus as the legal heir to the throne of David through Joseph. This was critical because the official genealogical records were housed in the Temple. By recording this before AD 70, Matthew preserved the legal right of Messiahship before the records were permanently destroyed by the Roman legions.

2) The "Three Fourteens" is a countdown clock

The structure of "three sets of fourteen" is not merely a memory aid; it is a theological timeline. Matthew divides Israel's history into three epochs: Foundation (Abraham to David), Monarchy (David to Exile), and Decline (Exile to Messiah). The third set ends with Jesus, indicating that the period of Decline and Exile is officially over. The "fullness of time" has arrived. There is no fourth set of fourteen; Jesus is the terminal point of the Old Covenant line and the beginning of the New Creation.

3) The Virgin Birth solves a legal crisis

The inclusion of Jeconiah (v. 11) presents a massive legal problem. In Jeremiah 22:30, God pronounced a curse on Jeconiah's bloodline, stating that no descendant of his would ever prosper sitting on the throne of David. This created a paradox: the Messiah must come from the royal line, but the royal line was cursed. The Virgin Birth is the divine solution. By being born of Mary (biological) but adopted by Joseph (legal), Jesus secures the legal right to the throne without inheriting the blood-curse of Jeconiah.

4) Saving "His People" from the Covenant Curse

The angel’s instruction in verse 21 defines the mission of the Messiah with precision: "He shall save His people from their sins." In the context of the first century, this is not a generic promise of going to heaven. It is the resolution of the "Great Divorce." Israel was in exile (spiritual and political) because of her sins—specifically, the violation of the Mosaic Covenant. Jesus comes as the Greater Joshua ("Yahweh Saves") to pay the debt of the Old Covenant, ending the curse of the Law, and leading the Remnant out of the wilderness of the Old Age.

5) Immanuel replaces the physical Temple

The citation of Isaiah 7:14 frames the entire book. "God with us" is the answer to the tragedy of the Exile, where God’s glory departed the Temple (Ezekiel 10). The arrival of Jesus means that God has returned to dwell with His people, but not in a building made of stone. Jesus Himself is the new meeting place between Heaven and Earth, signaling that the physical Temple is now obsolete and ready to vanish.

New Creation Reality

We do not wait for a Messiah to come; the genealogy is complete. We live on the other side of the "Three Fourteens." The silence of God has been permanently broken. We are the generation that lives in the reality of Immanuel—God permanently with us, no longer separated by veil, priest, or temple.

Reflection

Do I live as if God is "with us" right now, or am I still waiting for Him to show up? If Jesus resolved the "sins of His people" and ended the Exile, why do I often feel the burden of distance from God? The genealogy proves God keeps His appointments; am I trusting His timing in my own life?
