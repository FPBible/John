Matthew 1

Immediate Context

Matthew opens the Gospel account by anchoring the arrival of the Messiah in the legal and covenantal history of Israel. This chapter provides the bridge from the Old Covenant to the New Creation, documenting the royal lineage of Jesus and the miraculous nature of His entry into the world as the fulfillment of the Davidic and Abrahamic promises.

Scripture (LSV)

1The scroll of the genealogy of Jesus Christ, son of David, son of Abraham. 2Abraham begot Isaac, and Isaac begot Jacob, and Jacob begot Judah and his brothers, 3and Judah begot Pharez and Zarah of Tamar, and Pharez begot Hezron, and Hezron begot Ram, 4and Ram begot Amminadab, and Amminadab begot Nahshon, and Nahshon begot Salmon, 5and Salmon begot Boaz of Rahab, and Boaz begot Obed of Ruth, and Obed begot Jesse, 6and Jesse begot David the king. And David the king begot Solomon of her [who had been] Uriah’s, 7and Solomon begot Rehoboam, and Rehoboam begot Abijah, and Abijah begot Asa, 8and Asa begot Jehoshaphat, and Jehoshaphat begot Joram, and Joram begot Uzziah, 9and Uzziah begot Jotham, and Jotham begot Ahaz, and Ahaz begot Hezekiah, 10and Hezekiah begot Manasseh, and Manasseh begot Amon, and Amon begot Josiah, 11and Josiah begot Jeconiah and his brothers, at the Babylonian removal. 12And after the Babylonian removal, Jeconiah begot Shealtiel, and Shealtiel begot Zerubbabel, 13and Zerubbabel begot Abiud, and Abiud begot Eliakim, and Eliakim begot Azor, 14and Azor begot Zadok, and Zadok begot Achim, and Achim begot Eliud, 15and Eliud begot Eleazar, and Eleazar begot Matthan, and Matthan begot Jacob, 16and Jacob begot Joseph, the husband of Mary, of whom was begotten Jesus, who is named Christ. 17All the generations, then, from Abraham to David [are] fourteen generations, and from David to the Babylonian removal fourteen generations, and from the Babylonian removal to the Christ, fourteen generations. 18And of Jesus Christ, the birth was thus: for His mother Mary having been betrothed to Joseph, before their coming together she was found to have [conceived] in womb from the Holy Spirit, 19and Joseph her husband, being righteous, and not willing to make her an example, did wish privately to send her away. 20And on his thinking of these things, behold, a messenger of the LORD appeared to him in a dream, saying, “Joseph, son of David, you may not fear to receive Mary your wife, for that which was begotten in her is from the Holy Spirit, 21and she will bring forth a Son, and you will call His Name Jesus, for He will save His people from their sins.” 22And all this has come to pass, that it might be fulfilled that which was spoken by the LORD through the prophet, saying, 23“Behold, the virgin will have [conceived] in womb, and she will bring forth a Son, and they will call His Name Immanuel,” which is, being interpreted, “With us [is] God.” 24And Joseph, having risen from the sleep, did as the messenger of the LORD directed him, and received his wife, 25and did not know her until she brought forth her son—the first-born, and he called His Name Jesus.

Annotations

The scroll of the genealogy: (Gr. Biblos geneseos). A direct link to the creation account, signaling the start of the New Creation.
Jesus: Meaning "Yahweh is Salvation," identifying Him as the one who fulfills the Exodus type.
Fourteen generations: A gematric highlight of David’s name, emphasizing the royal authority of Christ.
He will save His people from their sins: The covenantal deliverance of Israel from the curse of the Law.
Immanuel: "God with us"—the transition from an external building to an internal, spiritual indwelling.

Fulfillment Commentary

1. The Genesis of the New Heavens and Earth

The Greek phrase Biblos geneseos in verse 1 is much more than a simple introduction to a family tree; it is a deliberate theological echo of Genesis 2:4 and 5:1. Matthew is announcing a "New Genesis." In the Full Preterist framework, this marks the official beginning of the transition from the Old Covenant age to the New Creation. The genealogy is not just a list of names; it is a legal document proving that the system of the Law has reached its climax in a single Person. For centuries, Israel had been defined by biological lineage and the physical land, but Matthew presents Jesus as the "Last Adam" who sums up the entire history of the Old World. This record identifies the one who would eventually dismantle the shadows of the Law and fully inaugurate the New Creation. By the time the Temple was destroyed in AD 70, the need for such physical genealogies was removed because the spiritual lineage of the Kingdom had been firmly established. We are no longer defined by the generations of the first Adam, but by the "scroll" of the King. This transition was the essential work of the first century—moving from a fleshly identification to a spiritual one. The genealogical records kept in the Temple served their purpose until the Messiah appeared; once the substance arrived, the shadows were legally ready to be dismissed.

2. The Sabbatical Climax of History

Matthew organizes the lineage into three sets of fourteen generations. This numerical arrangement is a sophisticated theological manifesto. In Hebrew gematria, the name "David" (D-V-D) equals 14. Matthew is shouting to his readers that Jesus is the "Thrice-Davidic" King, the ultimate heir to the throne. Furthermore, 3 sets of 14 equal 42 generations, which correlates precisely to the 42 encampments of Israel in the wilderness as recorded in the Torah. History has reached its destination; the wandering of the Old Covenant people is over. From a fulfilled perspective, this is the final "countdown" to the end of the Mosaic Age. The sabbatical rest that Israel could never find under the Law was now being offered in the person of Christ. This was the "fullness of time" mentioned by Paul, signaling that the "elements of the world" were about to be put away to make room for the eternal, spiritual Kingdom that we now inhabit as a settled reality. The structure of the genealogy proves that God is a God of order and timing; the Old Covenant was never meant to be permanent, but was a timed period of tutelage leading directly to the Christ. When the 42nd generation arrived, the "wilderness" period of the Law was legally terminated, and the entrance into the New Creation "Land" began.

3. The Removal of the Covenant Curse

When the messenger tells Joseph that Jesus will "save His people from their sins," it is vital to understand this in a first-century covenantal context rather than through the lens of modern individualistic pietism. "His people" refers specifically to Israel, and "their sins" specifically points to the corporate transgressions under the Law of Moses that had kept the nation in spiritual exile and under the threat of death. The Old Covenant could only identify and multiply sin; it could never truly remove it. Jesus came to fulfill the Law’s requirements and bear the curse of the Law on behalf of the remnant of Israel. This salvation was not a future-tense event for us today, but a historically completed act for the people of that age. It was inaugurated in Matthew 1, secured at the Cross, and fully consummated at the Parousia in AD 70 when the Law—which Paul called the "strength of sin"—was legally and physically retired along with the Temple system. Today, we do not live under the threat of the Mosaic curse or in a state of "waiting" for salvation to be finished. We live in the freedom of a salvation that is already finished and applied to the New Creation people. The "sins" that separated God from His people have been dealt with once for all, and the barrier of the Law has been removed forever.

4. Immanuel: The End of Localized Presence

The name "Immanuel" (God with us) signifies the most radical shift in human history: the transition from a localized presence in a stone temple to the indwelling presence of God in His people. Full Preterism recognizes that the true "Temple" is no longer a building in Jerusalem, but the corporate body of Christ. Matthew 1 introduces the concept that would be fully realized when the physical Temple in Jerusalem fell. The "with us" reality was no longer restricted by veils, human priests, or animal sacrifices. Because Christ fulfilled every shadow, the presence of God is now an immediate, direct, and constant reality for the believer. We live in the "New Jerusalem" where the tabernacle of God is with men (Rev 21:3). This wasn't a hope for a distant future; it was the entire purpose of Christ's entry into the world. In the New Creation, there is no more "distance" between God and His people, as the Old Covenant barriers have been permanently removed. The arrival of Immanuel meant that the "heaven and earth" of the old system had to pass away to make way for a world where God and man are perfectly reconciled. This reconciliation is not something we are waiting for in a second "coming"—it is the settled state of the New Creation which we currently inhabit. The presence of God is the air we breathe in the Kingdom.
