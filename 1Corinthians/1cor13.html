1 Corinthians 13

Love Remains When the Old World Passes

Original text: 1 Corinthians 13 — WEB

Fulfilled Paraphrase

Gifts Without Love Are Empty (13:1–3)

If I speak in the languages of humans and angels, but do not have love, I have become a noisy gong or a clanging cymbal.

If I have prophetic insight, understand all mysteries and all knowledge, and if I have all faith so as to move mountains, but do not have love, I am nothing.

If I give away everything I own, and even hand over my body to be burned, but do not have love, I gain nothing.

What Love Is — and Is Not (13:4–7)

Love is patient. Love is kind. Love does not envy. Love does not boast. Love is not inflated.

Love does not behave shamefully, does not seek its own advantage, is not easily provoked, and does not keep a record of wrongs.

Love does not rejoice in injustice, but rejoices with the truth.

Love bears all things, believes all things, hopes all things, endures all things.

What Has Ended and What Remains (13:8–13)

Love never fails. But prophecies have ceased. Tongues have fallen silent. Knowledge has passed away.

For we knew in part, and we prophesied in part,

but when the complete arrived, the partial passed away.

When I was a child, I spoke like a child, thought like a child, reasoned like a child. When I became mature, I set aside childish things.

For we once saw in a mirror dimly, but now face to face. Once I knew in part, but now I know fully, just as I am fully known.

So now remain faith, hope, and love — these three. And the greatest of these is love.

Immediate Context

Chapters 12–14 address spiritual gifts. Chapter 13 is not a poetic interruption — it is the interpretive centre. Paul contrasts temporary covenant tools with the permanent relational reality of the New Creation.

Fulfillment Commentary

1) “Love never fails” means it outlasts the old covenant structures

Paul contrasts love with gifts tied to a transitional age. Prophecy, tongues, and partial knowledge served a purpose during covenant change — but love is not temporary. It belongs to the completed order.

2) “The complete” is the arrived New Covenant reality

Paul does not point to a distant future. “The complete” refers to the maturity of New Covenant life in Christ — the full unveiling of what was partial under the Law. The paraphrase reflects this as fulfilled arrival.

3) “Face to face” is covenant clarity, not bodily sight

This echoes Old Testament language of relational access. It describes unmediated relationship, not physical eyesight. The veil has been removed; God is known directly in Christ.

4) Faith and hope remain — but love governs them

Faith and hope continue, but love is greater because it is the environment in which faith trusts and hope rests. Love is not one virtue among others — it is the atmosphere of the New Creation.

Key Language Notes

  • “Ceased / passed away” (13:8): completed transition, not future loss.
  • “The complete” (13:10): fulfilled covenant maturity.
  • “Face to face” (13:12): direct relational access, not physical vision.
  • “Remain” (13:13): what continues after transition is complete.

New Creation Reality

The New Creation is not held together by gifts, experiences, or knowledge. It is held together by love — patient, truthful, enduring love that reflects the finished work of Christ. Everything else served its purpose. Love remains.

Reflection

If love is the permanent reality, what temporary things am I still treating as if they were central?
