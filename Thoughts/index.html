“All These Things” — 70 AD, Resurrection, Judgment, and the Prophetic Test

A step-by-step response to the claim: “If Christ returned in 70 AD, where is the resurrection, judgment of the nations, end of death, and promised restoration?”

The objection:
“Jesus said when all these things happen. He did not say when some or most happen. If Christ ‘returned’ in 70 AD without a visible appearing, without the resurrection, and without judgment of the nations, that redefines His words after the fact.”

This response does not dismiss the seriousness of the claim. It challenges the assumptions being loaded into the words “coming,” “resurrection,” “judgment,” “death,” and “restoration.”

1) What does “all these things” actually refer to?

In the Olivet Discourse (Matthew 24, Mark 13, Luke 21), Jesus is responding to a conversation about the Temple and the crisis surrounding it.

  • “All these things” refers to the full set of signs Jesus just listed (wars, persecution, false christs, Jerusalem surrounded, tribulation, and the Temple’s desolation).
  • “All” does not mean “every prophecy in the Bible about all time.” It means “the complete package I am describing here.”

If someone expands “all these things” to include a modern end-of-the-planet scenario, they are importing a framework, not following the discourse.

2) What about the “coming of the Son of Man” — isn’t that a visible appearing?

“Coming on the clouds” is established prophetic language for judicial visitation and covenant judgment. In the Old Testament, God is described as “coming” against nations using cloud imagery, without implying a literal sky-body appearance.

The key question is not, “Does it match a modern literal expectation?” The question is: Did the covenantal judgment event Jesus predicted occur in the timeframe He stated?

The discourse is addressed to living disciples with direct “you will see” language. The audience and timeframe matter.

3) “Where is the resurrection?”

The objection assumes “resurrection” can only mean: “biological bodies reanimated worldwide, physical death immediately stops.” But Scripture uses “life/death/resurrection” in covenantal and corporate ways as well.

What the objection assumes

  • Graves open globally
  • Immortality begins physically for everyone
  • Resurrection is only biological

What a fulfilled framework argues

  • The “end of the age” resurrection is tied to the transition of covenant ages
  • The dead in Christ are raised into the fullness of the New Covenant reality
  • The living are gathered into that same reality (not excluded)

That’s why Paul can stress “order” (dead first, then living) in 1 Thessalonians 4—it’s about covenant inclusion and vindication, not camera footage.

You may disagree with this reading, but it must be refuted by exegesis (audience, idiom, genre), not by insisting on modern literal expectations.

4) “Where is the judgment of the nations?”

In Scripture, “nations” does not automatically mean “modern nation-states.” It often functions as covenantal language for “Gentiles” or surrounding peoples, and judgment frequently occurs through historical events.

  • The Olivet Discourse itself centers judgment heavily on Jerusalem’s leadership and the Temple system.
  • 70 AD was a public, catastrophic, historical judgment consistent with Jesus’ warnings.

5) “Where is the end of death?”

The objection assumes “death ended” must mean “no more physical funerals.” But in the New Testament, “death” is also a reigning covenantal power: condemnation, alienation, slavery under sin and law.

  • “Death destroyed” can mean: the power of death (condemnation) is broken permanently in Christ.
  • Physical death still occurs, but it no longer reigns as a covenant tyrant over God’s people.

6) “Where is the restoration promised by the prophets?”

The prophets describe restoration using covenant-world imagery: temple, land, Zion, cosmic signs. The New Testament repeatedly re-anchors those promises in Christ and the New Covenant:

  • Temple → God’s people
  • Inheritance/land → kingdom reality in Christ
  • Jerusalem/Zion → New Covenant community imagery
  • New creation → life in Christ now

The core question: Do you read the prophets through covenant idiom and apostolic application, or through modern cosmology/geopolitics imposed on the text?

7) The prophetic test (Deut 18): does it condemn a fulfilled reading?

Deuteronomy 18 condemns prophets whose words fail—not readers who misunderstand prophetic idiom. Jesus predicted specific things with specific timing markers:

  • Temple judgment language (e.g., “your house left desolate”)
  • Near-time urgency (“this generation” in the discourse context)
  • Siege and desolation markers (Luke 21’s “Jerusalem surrounded by armies”)

If those covenant events occurred as described in that generation, then the prophetic credibility stands. A fulfilled reading is not “redefining after the fact”; it is following audience, timeframe, and prophetic idiom.

8) When does reinterpretation become falsification?

It becomes falsification when you change the author’s intent to rescue a failed prediction. It is faithful interpretation when it follows:

  • Audience relevance (“you” language; first-century setting)
  • Time statements (“near,” “at hand,” “this generation”)
  • Old Testament prophetic idiom (cloud-coming judgment language)
  • Genre (apocalyptic and prophetic symbols)

The irony: the “falsification” charge often assumes futurism as the default dictionary, then accuses anyone who reads Scripture as Scripture reads itself.

Suggested reading (in the text itself)

  • Matthew 24 (Olivet Discourse)
  • Mark 13 (Olivet Discourse)
  • Luke 21 (“Jerusalem surrounded by armies” clarity)
  • 1 Thessalonians 4 (order: dead then living)
  • 1 Corinthians 15 (death, victory, resurrection language)
  • Deuteronomy 18 (prophetic test)
