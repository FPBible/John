Manifesto

1. Fulfillment, Not Expectation

We affirm that the “end of the age” spoken of by Jesus and the prophets was not the end of the physical universe, but the completion of Israel’s covenant age. That age reached its appointed conclusion in the first century, climaxing in the judgment of Jerusalem and the passing of the temple system.

This was not a failed expectation or a postponed promise. It was fulfillment. Christian faith does not stand on anticipation of unfinished prophecy, but on the reality that what was promised has been accomplished. The old covenant world has ended. A new covenant world has begun.

2. The Presence of Christ

The New Testament uses the word parousia to describe more than a momentary arrival. It speaks of presence — an abiding, royal nearness.

Christ is not absent from His kingdom. He has taken His throne. His reign did not begin in the future, nor is it delayed until another age. He reigns now, not as a distant ruler, but as a present Lord.

Christian life is therefore not lived in anticipation of Christ’s return, but in awareness of His nearness.

3. Life in the New Heavens and New Earth

Scripture describes the passing of the “former heaven and earth” as the removal of the old covenant order — a world structured around temple, priesthood, and ethnic boundary.

We now live in the New Heavens and New Earth: a renewed covenant reality where access to God is no longer mediated by place, ritual, or lineage. The barriers have been removed. The veil is gone. God dwells with His people.

This is not symbolic optimism. It is the present condition of Christian life.

4. The New Jerusalem

The New Jerusalem is not a future city descending from the sky. It is the living community of God’s people — a spiritual city, founded in heaven, expressed on earth.

Its gates are always open. Its life flows outward. Its purpose is healing, reconciliation, and life for the nations. To belong to Christ is to belong to this city now.

5. Resurrection and Life

The gospel proclaims victory over death — not merely biological death, but separation from God.

In Christ, death has lost its dominion. Resurrection life is not postponed until the end of history; it is the present inheritance of those who live in Him. Those who die in Christ do not wait in uncertainty. They rest in completed redemption.

Eternal life is not only future survival. It is present participation.

6. God All in All

With the fulfillment of Israel’s covenant mission, God’s relationship with humanity is no longer confined to a single nation or sacred geography.

We live in the age of God’s unveiled nearness — where worship is not bound to temples, where holiness is not confined to rituals, and where God’s presence fills ordinary life.

Faith is no longer about access. It is about awareness. No sacred ground is restricted. No moment is excluded. God is all in all.

A Living Analogy

For generations, the world lived under a provisional light — like a lantern used through the night. The prophets spoke of a sunrise that would make the lantern unnecessary.

That sunrise has come.

We are not waiting for the day to arrive. We are learning how to live in it.

Closing Statement
The New Creation is not an idea to defend. It is a reality to inhabit.
Christian faith, after fulfillment, is not anxious, delayed, or suspended between ages. It is grounded, present, and alive.

We do not wait for Christ.
We walk with Him.
