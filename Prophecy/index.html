Prophecy Hub

Fulfilled Judgment · Covenant Transition · New Creation

How to Read This Hub

Biblical prophecy does not describe the end of the physical universe. It announces covenant judgment, the removal of the Old Covenant order, and the establishment of the New Covenant world in Christ.

These pages explain how Scripture itself uses prophetic imagery and how the New Testament declares its fulfillment. They exist to clarify language, not to generate speculation or future timelines.

I. Covenant Judgment Texts (Announce the Transition)

These texts declare judgment on the Old Covenant system, using symbolic and apocalyptic language drawn from Israel’s Scriptures. They are audience-relevant, time-bound, and fulfilled within the first century.

  • Matthew 24 — Judgment on Jerusalem
  • Luke 21 — Days of Vengeance
  • Daniel — Kingdom, Son of Man, and Time
  • Isaiah — New Heavens, New Earth, New Humanity
  • Ezekiel 37 — Resurrection as Covenant Restoration
  • Psalms — Enthronement and Rule
  • 2 Peter 3 — Heavens and Earth as Covenant Transition
  • Revelation — Symbol, Judgment, Victory

II. Presence and Parousia Clarified

These texts explain how Christ’s “coming” is described in Scripture — not as physical descent or delayed expectation, but as royal presence, covenant vindication, and exercised authority.

  • 1 Thessalonians 4 — Presence, Not Escape

III. Resurrection Explained (Interpretive Framework)

These writings are not prophetic announcements. They define and interpret resurrection in light of Christ’s finished work and the completed covenant transition.

  • 1 Corinthians 15 — Resurrection and Victory

IV. Core Covenant Themes (Support Pages)

These pages support chapter commentary where covenant language appears repeatedly. They prevent unnecessary repetition and keep chapters focused and readable.

  • New Creation — Fulfilled Covenant Reality
  • New Covenant — Internalized Law, Direct Access, New Creation Life
  • Temple — Judgment on the Old Center, Replacement in Christ
  • Wilderness — Exodus Pattern, Testing, and Transition

Important Note

Prophetic symbolism must be interpreted by Scripture itself. Where the New Testament explains resurrection, judgment, kingdom, or new creation, those explanations govern how prophetic language is read.
