Prophecy Hub

Fulfilled Judgment · Covenant Transition · New Creation

The Bible’s prophetic language does not describe the end of the physical universe. It announces covenant judgment, the removal of the Old Covenant order, and the establishment of the New Covenant world in Christ.

This hub gathers key prophetic texts and language frameworks that explain how Scripture itself uses imagery such as “coming,” “judgment,” “resurrection,” “heaven and earth,” and “the Day.”

Guiding principle: Prophecy must be interpreted by Scripture itself. Where the New Testament explains prophetic language, those explanations govern how the prophets are read.

I. Covenant Judgment Texts

These texts announce the covenant transition using symbolic and apocalyptic language drawn from Israel’s Scriptures. They are audience-relevant, time-bound, and fulfilled within the first century.

  • Matthew 24 — Judgment on Jerusalem
  • Luke 21 — Days of Vengeance
  • Daniel — Kingdom, Son of Man, and Time
  • Isaiah — New Heavens, New Earth, New Humanity
  • Ezekiel 37 — Resurrection as Restoration
  • Psalms — Enthronement and Rule
  • 2 Peter 3 — Heavens and Earth
  • Revelation — Symbol, Judgment, Victory

II. Presence and Parousia Clarified

These texts explain how Christ’s “coming” is described in Scripture — not as physical escape or delay, but as royal presence, covenant vindication, and historical judgment on the old order.

  • 1 Thessalonians 4 — Presence, Not Escape

III. Resurrection Explained

These writings define and interpret resurrection in the light of Christ’s finished work and the completed covenant transition.

  • 1 Corinthians 15 — Resurrection and Victory

IV. Core Prophetic Language Guides

These framework pages clarify how prophetic language functions in Scripture. They are referenced throughout the Bible commentary to maintain consistency and prevent modern assumptions.

  • Day of the Lord — Historical Judgment Vocabulary

V. Identity, Adversary, and Judgment Clarified

These pages address commonly misunderstood concepts that are often imported with modern or non-biblical assumptions.
