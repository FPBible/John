Satan

Adversary · Accuser · Apostate Authority

In Scripture, “satan” begins as a role: adversary, accuser, prosecutor — covenant courtroom language. In the first-century crisis, this adversarial power is embodied in an apostate hierarchy that condemns and kills. Christ ends the condemning jurisdiction, takes His throne, and establishes the New Covenant as present reality.

Definition & Function

“Satan” language is not introduced in the Bible as a cosmic biography with an origin story. It is introduced as a covenantal-legal function: opposition, accusation, and prosecution in settings where standing and judgment are in view.

By the New Testament era, Satanas may read like a proper name, but the controlling feature remains accusation with authority. That accusing power is then placed on a historical timeline and brought to an end through Christ in the covenant transition (AD 30–70).

How FPBible Reads “Satan”

  • Identify the function: accusation, slander, obstruction, persecution, testing.
  • Identify the arena: covenant court, priesthood, leadership, governance.
  • Ask what grants the accusation standing.
  • Fix the text within the AD 30–70 covenant transition.
  • Read apocalyptic imagery as authority language, not cosmology.

Old Testament Usage

The Hebrew satan means adversary or accuser. It is a role-word, not an explanation of supernatural origins. This is why “satan” can describe human opposition in some passages and formal prosecution in others.

Job and Covenant Court Language

In Job 1–2, the satan appears as prosecutor. He challenges righteousness and presses a case within a covenantal authority frame. The narrative emphasises limits: the accuser is not sovereign; God remains judge.

Sources: Job 1 (WEB), Job 2 (WEB)

Zechariah and Restored Standing

Zechariah 3 presents covenant courtroom imagery: the high priest stands defiled while the accuser resists restoration. God rebukes the accuser, changes garments, and restores standing. Accusation is silenced so renewal can proceed.

Source: Zechariah 3 (WEB)

The Accusing System in Jesus’ Day

By the first century, adversary language is embodied in an apostate leadership system that governs through accusation and condemnation. The Gospel narratives read like trials because the conflict is legal and covenantal.

Christ and the End of Condemnation

The accuser’s authority depends on jurisdiction. Christ ends that jurisdiction by fulfilling the old order and establishing permanent access. Where condemnation is removed, accusation loses legal force.

Sources: John 12 (WEB), John 16 (WEB), Romans 8 (WEB)

Revelation and Dethronement

Revelation 12 defines the dragon as “the accuser.” Being “cast down” is the loss of covenant standing and authority. This depicts the removal of the persecuting apostate system during the covenant transition, not a future cosmic conflict.

Source: Revelation 12 (WEB)

New Creation Implications

New Covenant life is not lived under a prosecutorial cloud. Believers live from settled access and standing, not ongoing accusation. This does not remove discernment or correction; it removes the condemning authority that weaponises accusation.
