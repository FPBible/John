Life

Eternal Life as Present Covenant Reality

In Scripture, life is not defined by biological function, future location, or post-mortem speculation. It is defined by access, union, and participation in God.

This page governs how “life”, “eternal life”, “alive”, “living”, and “perish” language is read throughout FPBible.

1. Life Is a Covenant Category Before It Is a Biological One

From the beginning of Scripture, life is framed relationally. Adam is created alive biologically, yet Scripture measures his life by presence with God, not by respiration.

When Adam is expelled from the garden, he does not cease to exist. But he is cut off from the place of life. The biblical pattern is established early:

  • Life = access, communion, inheritance
  • Death = exile, separation, condemnation

This covenant meaning governs Israel’s story. To be “alive” is to belong, to dwell, to stand before God. To be “cut off” is to be dead — even while breathing.

2. Life Under the Law Was Conditional and Fragile

Under the Mosaic Covenant, life was always conditional. It depended on obedience, sacrifice, priesthood, and temple mediation.

This is why Scripture repeatedly speaks of Israel as:

  • living yet “dead”
  • near yet excluded
  • chosen yet condemned

The Law could define life, but it could not give life. It could promise blessing, but it could not secure permanence. It produced a people who were always: alive by provision, not by union.

This tension is deliberate. The Law exposes the problem. It does not solve it.

3. Jesus Does Not Offer Life Later — He Announces Life Arriving

When Jesus speaks of life and eternal life, He does so to people who are physically alive. That alone should settle the issue.

“Eternal life” is not defined by duration, but by source and quality. It is the life of the age to come — the life of the New Covenant — breaking into the present.

Jesus repeatedly locates life in:

  • knowing the Father
  • abiding in Him
  • eating and drinking His life
  • believing and passing from death into life

None of this language points forward to a post-death reward. It announces a transfer of covenant status from death to life in the present.

4. Eternal Life Is Life Beyond Condemnation

Throughout the New Testament, life is consistently contrasted not with physical dying, but with condemnation.

This is decisive. If “life” meant “never biologically dying,” Paul’s arguments collapse. Bodies still die. Yet Paul insists believers already possess life.

The contrast is covenantal:

  • Death — under Law, accusation, exile
  • Life — in Christ, justified, reconciled

Life is the condition of being no longer under judgment. It is the state of standing accepted, not threatened.

5. Life in the Spirit Replaces Life Under the Law

Paul’s language is exact. Life is now located in the Spirit, not in the flesh, not in Torah identity, not in ethnic or covenant boundary markers.

To live “according to the Spirit” does not mean mystical intensity. It means living from a new covenant location:

  • no mediator required
  • no sacrifice pending
  • no condemnation hanging overhead

Life in the Spirit is not something to achieve. It is something to recognise and walk from.

This is why New Testament exhortation flows from identity, not threat. Life is assumed, not dangled.

6. “Perishing” Language Does Not Cancel Life

Biblical warnings about “perishing” are covenantal, not metaphysical. They describe loss, judgment, and exclusion within the Old Covenant world — especially in the AD 30–70 transition period.

They do not redefine life as uncertain or fragile. They warn that rejecting Christ during that period meant remaining in a world that was passing away.

Once that world ended, the framework closed. Life is no longer something to miss by timing. It is the settled reality of the New Creation.

7. What Life Means Now

To say believers have life now is not poetry. It is a theological claim with consequences.

  • Life is not interrupted by death
  • Life is not suspended awaiting judgment
  • Life is not fragile or provisional
  • Life is not maintained by fear

Life is participation in Christ’s own standing before the Father. It is the shared life of the Son.

This does not deny grief, loss, or mortality. It denies that any of those undo union.

8. Life as the Governing Reality

If life is misunderstood, the gospel collapses into:

  • delay theology
  • fear-based motivation
  • endless introspection
  • unfinished redemption

If life is understood covenantally:

  • assurance replaces anxiety
  • obedience flows from freedom
  • holiness becomes expression, not survival
  • faith becomes lived trust, not future hope

Summary

  • Life in Scripture is relational and covenantal
  • Life under the Law was conditional and fragile
  • Jesus announces life arriving, not life delayed
  • Eternal life is present participation, not post-death timing
  • Life in the Spirit replaces life under condemnation
