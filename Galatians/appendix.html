Galatians — Appendix

Key Themes, Terms, and Covenant Framework

This appendix provides orientation for reading Galatians clearly. It is not a running commentary. It highlights the covenant framework, key terms, and recurring contrasts that govern Paul’s argument.

1) Law as Covenant System

In Galatians, “law” does not mean generic moral instruction. It refers to the Mosaic covenant as a governing system — including circumcision, calendar observance, boundary markers, and the legal framework that defined Israel’s identity.

Paul’s argument is not that the law was bad, but that its role was temporary. Once Christ has come, the law no longer functions as a covenant authority.

2) Promise Versus Law

Paul contrasts promise and law, not as two spiritual techniques, but as two covenant foundations.

Promise precedes law, and promise is fulfilled in Christ. Law cannot inherit; only promise does.

This is why returning to the law is not spiritual caution — it is covenant reversal.

3) Curse Language

Galatians uses “curse” language drawn from Deuteronomy. This is covenant language, not mystical doom.

To be “under a curse” is to be under a covenant that condemns when its terms are violated. Christ redeems from this curse by ending the covenant that generated it.

4) Seed and Inheritance

Paul’s use of “seed” (singular) is not wordplay for its own sake. It is a covenant argument.

Inheritance is not transmitted through ethnicity or law-keeping, but through union with the promised Seed — Christ. Those in Christ share the inheritance, regardless of ethnic or legal status.

5) Slave and Son

Galatians contrasts slavery and sonship. This is not psychological language. It is covenant status.

The slave operates under obligation. The son operates from belonging. To return to the law is to return to slavery, even if it feels religious.

6) Flesh and Spirit

“Flesh” in Galatians is not simply bodily weakness. It refers to life oriented around the old covenant order — self-effort, boundary maintenance, and identity derived from law.

“Spirit” refers to life animated by the New Covenant — direct relationship, inward transformation, and shared life in Christ.

7) Freedom in Galatians

Freedom is not autonomy. It is release from a system that cannot give life.

Galatians insists that freedom is the normal state of those in Christ. To submit again to the law is to misunderstand what freedom is.

8) New Creation

Galatians reaches its conclusion in a single verdict:

“Neither circumcision nor uncircumcision means anything. What counts is new creation.”

New Creation is not a future hope here. It is the present covenant reality that replaces all former identity markers.

How to Use This Appendix

If Galatians feels confrontational, that is intentional. Paul is defending the integrity of the gospel.

Use this appendix to keep categories clear: law vs promise, slave vs son, flesh vs Spirit, old covenant vs New Creation.

When those lines stay clear, Galatians reads not as harsh, but as liberating.
